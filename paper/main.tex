\title{Transcender: Cross-Layer Composition Control for Adaptive Transformer Inference}
\author{Beomjune Son}
\date{March 2026}

\begin{document}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We present Transcender, a cross-layer composition control framework for adaptive transformer inference. The central thesis is that adaptive-depth inference is constrained less by exit-point selection than by whether intermediate-depth and full-depth signals can be combined without corrupting the output distribution. We evaluate this framing across three empirical tracks, producing a mixed but coherent result. On GPT-OSS 20B (Track~A), \texttt{top1\_agree} composition at Layer~22 with entropy-gated exit achieves 0.969 exact match against full-depth greedy decoding while physically skipping approximately 49.5\% of layers. Track~B provides a scoped negative comparison: a naive Gemma~3 4B-IT to GPT-OSS 20B cross-model cascade reaches only 0.026 exact match at 0.28 tokens per second (TPS) on the local Apple MLX runtime. Track~C extends the evaluation to dense architectures. On Gemma~3 4B-IT, compute-both agreement-aware composition recovers 0.807 exact match at both L31 and L20, versus 0.198 and 0.026 for fixed exit at those layers, while real selective-depth remains practically weak. Focused late-checkpoint follow-up on Llama~3.1 8B and Mistral~7B reproduces the same pattern: fixed exit fails materially, compute-both \texttt{top1\_agree} recovers full-depth output, and real selective-depth still trails the matched full-depth baseline. The resulting picture is architecture-dependent: strong practical adaptive depth on the tested MoE model, but only partial methodology transfer to the tested dense families.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Variable-compute inference for transformer language models is commonly framed in one of two ways: early exit within a single model~\cite{elbayad2020depthadaptive}, or draft-verifier decoding across models~\cite{leviathan2023speculative}. In both cases, the central question is typically \emph{when} the model has done enough work to emit the next token. We argue that this framing is incomplete.

The deeper problem is \emph{cross-layer composition control}: when an intermediate layer and the final layer disagree, a system needs a principled rule for deciding whether to trust the shallow path, the deep path, or a combination of the two. Without such a rule, adaptive-depth inference either collapses into low-quality fixed exit or pays the full cost of computing both paths without recovering a practical speed-quality tradeoff.

We present evidence supporting this reframing across three tracks. Track~A establishes a practical same-model adaptive-depth frontier on GPT-OSS 20B. Track~B demonstrates that a naive cross-model cascade can fail severely when tokenizer mismatch, distribution mismatch, and orchestration overhead compound. Track~C characterizes both the promise and the current limits of composition control on dense models: compute-both agreement-aware composition recovers substantial quality on Gemma~3 4B-IT, Llama~3.1 8B, and Mistral~7B, but real selective-depth implementations do not yet beat their matched full-depth baselines on this runtime.

\section{Contributions}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{The Subspace Paradox.} We show that hidden states from different transformer layers occupy geometrically separated subspaces, making hidden-state interpolation structurally invalid for cross-layer composition. Measured separation ratios reach 4.11$\times$ on GPT-OSS 20B and 2.46$\times$ on Gemma~3 4B-IT.

\item \textbf{Agreement-aware logit-space composition.} We propose a conservative composition rule that blends logits only when the shallow and deep paths agree on token identity. This outperforms both fixed exit and naive blending across the tested MoE and dense architectures.

\item \textbf{Empirical characterization of the cascade tax.} We measure the compounding cost of tokenizer mismatch, distribution mismatch, and two-model orchestration in a naive cross-model cascade, providing a concrete negative baseline.

\item \textbf{Family-sensitive dense depth profiles.} We observe that KL-divergence depth profiles differ across dense model families: Gemma exhibits a late plateau (L20--L29), Llama a milder middle plateau (L12--L14), and Mistral an earlier plateau (L2--L8). The benchmark-side limitation generalizes more cleanly than the internal profile geometry.

\item \textbf{Real dense selective-depth speed validation.} We implement continuation-safe selective-depth runtimes on three dense model families. All physically skip deep layers on some decode tokens, but none recovers a practical speed-quality frontier on this runtime.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Method}

\subsection{Cross-Layer Geometric Separation}

Let $h_i$ and $h_j$ denote hidden states from layers $i$ and $j$ with $i < j$. When projected into a shared PCA space, we measure cross-layer separation using
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{SeparationRatio}(i,j) =
\frac{\lVert c_i - c_j \rVert_2}{\max(\sigma_i,\sigma_j)},
\label{eq:separation}
\end{equation}
where $c_i$ and $c_j$ are cluster centroids and $\sigma_i$, $\sigma_j$ are within-cluster standard deviations. Ratios exceeding 2$\times$ indicate that hidden states from different layers are not safely interpolable; we observe 4.11$\times$ on GPT-OSS 20B and 2.46$\times$ on Gemma~3 4B-IT.

\subsection{Agreement-Aware Logit Composition}

Rather than blending hidden states, Transcender projects intermediate and final representations into a shared logit space and composes them there. Let $\ell_t^e$ denote the logits at exit layer $e$ for token step $t$, and $\ell_t^N$ the logits at full depth $N$. The composition rule is
\begin{equation}
\ell_t^{\mathrm{out}} =
\begin{cases}
(1-\alpha)\,\ell_t^N + \alpha\,\ell_t^e, &
\text{if } \arg\max \ell_t^e = \arg\max \ell_t^N, \\
\ell_t^N, & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:blend}
\end{equation}
We refer to this as the \texttt{top1\_agree} rule. Its design is conservative: logits are blended only when the shallow and deep paths already agree on token identity; otherwise, the shallow signal is discarded entirely.

\subsection{Selective-Depth Runtime}

The dense selective-depth runtime used in Track~C differs from the compute-both composition path. Prompt prefill always runs at full depth to ensure deep-cache validity at decode start. During autoregressive decoding, layers $0 \ldots e$ execute first, a confidence score $s(\ell_t^e)$ is computed, and the early token is accepted only when confidence exceeds a threshold:
\begin{equation}
a_t = \mathbf{1}[s(\ell_t^e) \ge \tau], \qquad
\hat{y}_t =
\begin{cases}
\arg\max \ell_t^e, & a_t = 1, \\
\arg\max \ell_t^N, & a_t = 0.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:selective}
\end{equation}
When continuation is required after one or more accepted shallow tokens, the previously skipped tokens are replayed through the deep layers to restore cache correctness. This replay cost is included in all reported runtimes and is essential to the interpretation of the results.

\section{Experimental Setup}

All results were collected on a single Apple M1 Pro system with 32~GB unified memory using the Apple MLX runtime. The evaluation uses a fixed prompt suite of five factual and explanatory prompts covering quantum entanglement, the French Revolution, recursion, TCP versus UDP, and photosynthesis. Each prompt generates 48 tokens under greedy decoding. Aggregate metrics exclude the first prompt as warmup to reduce distortion from JIT compilation and initial memory allocation.

The primary quality metric is exact token-sequence match against a full-depth baseline on the same model. Secondary metrics include generation throughput (tokens per second), time to first token (TTFT), elapsed time, peak memory, and layer-saving statistics where applicable.

The evaluated models are: GPT-OSS 20B, a 24-layer Mixture-of-Experts model in MXFP4 quantization; Gemma~3 4B-IT~\cite{gemma2024report}, a 34-layer dense model; and two MLX 4-bit dense models---Llama~3.1 8B Instruct and Mistral~7B Instruct v0.3. Track~B combines Gemma~3 4B-IT and GPT-OSS 20B in a naive cross-model cascade. Track~C evaluates Gemma~3 4B-IT across six adaptive modes and dedicated selective-depth runtimes, then extends to late-checkpoint validation on Llama and Mistral.

\section{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Architecture sensitivity.} The strongest practical result appears on the tested MoE model. The magnitude of adaptive-depth gains is architecture-dependent.

\item \textbf{Track B scope.} The cascade result is specific to a naive implementation, one model pair, and one local runtime. It should not be generalized to speculative decoding as a class of methods.

\item \textbf{Runtime dependence.} All throughput, latency, and memory measurements are specific to a single Apple M1 Pro system using MLX. Direct comparison to GPU-based inference is not appropriate.

\item \textbf{Evaluation scale.} The evaluation uses five prompts with warmup exclusion. This is sufficient for directional findings but insufficient for statistical confidence intervals.

\item \textbf{Dense selective-depth.} Real selective-depth runtimes on all three tested dense families were operationally correct but did not recover a practical speed-quality frontier in this configuration.

\item \textbf{Continuation mechanisms.} The current selective-depth results demonstrate that checkpoint placement and late-checkpoint confidence alone are insufficient. Better continuation criteria, token-difficulty-aware routing, and lower-overhead cache repair remain open problems.

\item \textbf{Family-sensitive structure.} The benchmark-side dense limitation generalizes across tested families, but the internal KL-profile shape does not. We do not claim a single universal dense depth profile.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

The evidence presented supports a narrow but consistent conclusion: adaptive-depth inference for transformers is constrained less by exit-point selection than by cross-layer composition control---how intermediate and full-depth signals are combined without corrupting the output distribution.

Track~A remains the primary result. On GPT-OSS 20B, \texttt{top1\_agree} at Layer~22 with entropy-gated exit achieves 0.969 exact match with approximately 49.5\% real layer savings. Track~B provides a scoped negative baseline for this naive cascade configuration. Track~C contributes three findings: fixed-exit failure on dense models, strong compute-both composition recovery, and weak real selective-depth across three tested families.

The dense-model conclusion is not that adaptive depth is impossible, but that neither fixed exit nor current selective-depth mechanisms establish a practical frontier on the tested dense architectures. The next research direction is correspondingly narrower: whether family-sensitive continuation criteria, token-difficulty-aware routing, and cache-aware continuation can close the gap. That remains to be validated.
