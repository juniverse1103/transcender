\title{Transcender: Cross-Layer Composition Control for Adaptive Transformer Inference}
\author{Beomjune Son}
\date{March 2026}

\begin{abstract}
Adaptive-depth inference is increasingly relevant for on-device transformer deployment, yet its practical limits on consumer hardware remain undercharacterized. We argue that the binding constraint is not exit-point selection alone, but how intermediate-depth and full-depth signals are composed without corrupting the output distribution. We present Transcender, a cross-layer composition control framework for adaptive transformer inference, and evaluate this framing across three empirical tracks on a single Apple M1 Pro system using the MLX runtime, producing a mixed but coherent result. On two sparse MoE architectures (Track~A), \texttt{top1\_agree} composition at the penultimate layer with entropy-gated exit achieves 0.870 exact match on GPT-OSS 20B (47 of 63 prompts perfect) and 0.837 on Qwen3-30B-A3B (36 of 63 perfect), each with a sharp quality cliff one layer earlier. Track~B provides a scoped negative comparison: a naive Gemma~3 4B-IT to GPT-OSS 20B cross-model cascade reaches only 0.026 exact match at 0.28 tokens per second (TPS) on the local Apple MLX runtime. Track~C extends the evaluation to dense architectures. On Gemma~3 4B-IT, compute-both agreement-aware composition recovers 0.807 exact match at both L31 and L20, versus 0.198 and 0.026 for fixed exit at those layers, while real selective-depth remains practically weak. Focused late-checkpoint follow-up on Llama~3.1 8B and Mistral~7B reproduces the same pattern: fixed exit fails materially, compute-both \texttt{top1\_agree} recovers full-depth output, and real selective-depth still trails the matched full-depth baseline. The resulting picture is architecture-dependent: a viable penultimate-layer selective-exit frontier on both tested sparse MoE families, but no practical selective-depth frontier on the tested dense architectures.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Variable-compute inference for transformer language models is commonly framed in one of two ways: early exit within a single model~\cite{elbayad2020depthadaptive}, or draft-verifier decoding across models~\cite{leviathan2023speculative}. In both cases, the central question is typically \emph{when} the model has done enough work to emit the next token. We argue that this framing is incomplete.

This question has particular practical relevance for consumer-hardware deployment, where transformer models run on devices with constrained unified memory, batch size is typically one, and inference latency directly determines user experience. Adaptive-depth mechanisms that reduce per-token compute without a secondary model or server-side orchestration are of direct interest in such settings. All experiments in this work were conducted on a single Apple M1 Pro system with 32~GB unified memory using the Apple MLX runtime.

We argue that the binding constraint in the tested setting is not exit-point selection alone, but \emph{cross-layer composition control}: when an intermediate layer and the final layer disagree, a system needs a principled rule for deciding whether to trust the shallow path, the deep path, or a combination of the two. Without such a rule, adaptive-depth inference either collapses into low-quality fixed exit or pays the full cost of computing both paths without recovering a practical speed-quality tradeoff.

We present evidence supporting this reframing across three tracks. Track~A identifies a same-model adaptive-depth operating point on GPT-OSS 20B, with cross-model reproduction on Qwen3-30B-A3B. Track~B demonstrates that a naive cross-model cascade can fail severely when tokenizer mismatch, distribution mismatch, and orchestration overhead compound. Track~C characterizes both the promise and the current limits of composition control on dense models: compute-both agreement-aware composition recovers substantial quality on Gemma~3 4B-IT, Llama~3.1 8B, and Mistral~7B, but real selective-depth implementations do not yet beat their matched full-depth baselines on this runtime.

\section{Contributions}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Consumer-hardware adaptive-depth characterization.} We provide a multi-family empirical characterization of adaptive-depth inference on a consumer Apple Silicon system (M1~Pro, 32~GB unified memory), reporting both quality and runtime measurements across five models and two architecture classes under the Apple MLX runtime.

\item \textbf{Cross-layer subspace mismatch.} We measure that hidden states from different transformer layers occupy geometrically separated subspaces, indicating that naive hidden-state interpolation is empirically unsupported as a composition mechanism. Measured separation ratios reach 4.11$\times$ on GPT-OSS 20B and 2.46$\times$ on Gemma~3 4B-IT.

\item \textbf{Agreement-aware logit-space composition.} We propose a conservative composition rule that blends logits only when the shallow and deep paths agree on token identity. This outperforms both fixed exit and naive blending across the tested MoE and dense architectures.

\item \textbf{Empirical characterization of the cascade tax.} We measure the compounding cost of tokenizer mismatch, distribution mismatch, and two-model orchestration in a naive cross-model cascade, providing a concrete negative baseline.

\item \textbf{Family-sensitive dense depth profiles.} We observe that KL-divergence depth profiles differ across dense model families: Gemma exhibits a late plateau (L20--L29), Llama a milder middle plateau (L12--L14), and Mistral an earlier plateau (L2--L8). The benchmark-side limitation generalizes more cleanly than the internal profile geometry.

\item \textbf{Real dense selective-depth speed validation.} We implement continuation-safe selective-depth runtimes on three dense model families. All physically skip deep layers on some decode tokens, but none recovers a practical speed-quality frontier on this runtime.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

Adaptive-depth inference for transformers has been studied along several axes. Elbayad et al.~\cite{elbayad2020depthadaptive} introduce per-token depth allocation via learned halting classifiers. CALM~\cite{schuster2022calm} extends this with calibrated confidence thresholds, demonstrating early exit on encoder-decoder models with a learned consistency mechanism. DeeBERT~\cite{xin2020deebert} applies early exit to BERT classification tasks using entropy-based halting. These approaches focus on \emph{when} to exit; the present work shifts emphasis to \emph{how} to compose intermediate and full-depth signals when the exit decision is uncertain.

Speculative decoding~\cite{leviathan2023speculative, chen2023speculative} addresses a related problem from the opposite direction: a small draft model proposes tokens, and a larger verifier accepts or rejects them. LayerSkip~\cite{elhoushi2024layerskip} combines early exit with self-speculative decoding within a single model, using early layers as the draft and later layers as the verifier. Track~B's cascade result is a scoped negative baseline for a naive cross-model configuration, not a comparison to production speculative decoding systems.

Mixture-of-Depths~\cite{raposo2024mod} trains transformers with per-token depth routing, allowing tokens to skip entire layers during forward passes. This is structurally related to Track~A's entropy-gated exit but differs in requiring training-time routing decisions rather than post-hoc inference-time composition. Switch Transformers~\cite{fedus2022switch} demonstrate that sparse expert routing enables efficient scaling; the MoE structure in Track~A's tested models creates natural per-token compute variation that may interact with adaptive-depth mechanisms differently than dense architectures, a distinction that Track~C's results empirically support.

The present work differs from the above primarily in emphasis. Rather than proposing a new exit criterion or training procedure, we characterize the empirical operating limits of composition control across architecture classes and report both positive and negative results on consumer hardware.

\section{Method}

\subsection{Cross-Layer Subspace Mismatch}

Let $h_i$ and $h_j$ denote hidden states from layers $i$ and $j$ with $i < j$. When projected into a shared PCA space, we measure cross-layer separation using
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{SeparationRatio}(i,j) =
\frac{\lVert c_i - c_j \rVert_2}{\max(\sigma_i,\sigma_j)},
\label{eq:separation}
\end{equation}
where $c_i$ and $c_j$ are cluster centroids and $\sigma_i$, $\sigma_j$ are within-cluster standard deviations. Ratios exceeding 2$\times$ suggest that hidden states from different layers are geometrically misaligned, making naive interpolation between them empirically unstable. We observe 4.11$\times$ on GPT-OSS 20B and 2.46$\times$ on Gemma~3 4B-IT under our setup.

\subsection{Agreement-Aware Logit Composition}

Given the observed cross-layer subspace mismatch, Transcender avoids hidden-state blending and instead projects intermediate and final representations into a shared logit space for composition. Let $\ell_t^e$ denote the logits at exit layer $e$ for token step $t$, and $\ell_t^N$ the logits at full depth $N$. The composition rule is
\begin{equation}
\ell_t^{\mathrm{out}} =
\begin{cases}
(1-\alpha)\,\ell_t^N + \alpha\,\ell_t^e, &
\text{if } \arg\max \ell_t^e = \arg\max \ell_t^N, \\
\ell_t^N, & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:blend}
\end{equation}
We refer to this as the \texttt{top1\_agree} rule. Its design is conservative: logits are blended only when the shallow and deep paths already agree on token identity; otherwise, the shallow signal is discarded entirely.

\subsection{Selective-Depth Runtime}

The dense selective-depth runtime used in Track~C differs from the compute-both composition path. Prompt prefill always runs at full depth to ensure deep-cache validity at decode start. During autoregressive decoding, layers $0 \ldots e$ execute first, a confidence score $s(\ell_t^e)$ is computed, and the early token is accepted only when confidence exceeds a threshold:
\begin{equation}
a_t = \mathbf{1}[s(\ell_t^e) \ge \tau], \qquad
\hat{y}_t =
\begin{cases}
\arg\max \ell_t^e, & a_t = 1, \\
\arg\max \ell_t^N, & a_t = 0.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:selective}
\end{equation}
When continuation is required after one or more accepted shallow tokens, the previously skipped tokens are replayed through the deep layers to restore cache correctness. This replay cost is included in all reported runtimes and is essential to the interpretation of the results.

\section{Experimental Setup}

All results were collected on a single Apple M1 Pro system with 32~GB unified memory using the Apple MLX runtime. The Track~A evaluation uses a suite of 64 prompts spanning six categories: expository (13), code/technical (10), reasoning/logic (10), creative/open-ended (10), short-answer factual (11), and list/structured output (10). Each prompt generates 48 tokens under greedy decoding. Aggregate metrics exclude the first prompt as warmup to reduce distortion from JIT compilation and initial memory allocation, yielding $N{=}63$ scored prompts. Tracks~B and~C use a smaller five-prompt subset of the expository category.

The primary quality metric is exact token-sequence match against a full-depth baseline on the same model. Secondary metrics include generation throughput (tokens per second), time to first token (TTFT), elapsed time, peak memory, and layer-saving statistics where applicable.

The evaluated models are: GPT-OSS 20B, a 24-layer Mixture-of-Experts model (32 experts, top-4) in MXFP4 quantization; Qwen3-30B-A3B, a 48-layer MoE model (128 experts, top-8) in 4-bit quantization; Gemma~3 4B-IT~\cite{gemma2024report}, a 34-layer dense model; and two MLX 4-bit dense models---Llama~3.1 8B Instruct and Mistral~7B Instruct v0.3. Track~A evaluates both MoE models. Track~B combines Gemma~3 4B-IT and GPT-OSS 20B in a naive cross-model cascade. Track~C evaluates Gemma~3 4B-IT across six adaptive modes and dedicated selective-depth runtimes, then extends to late-checkpoint validation on Llama and Mistral.

Qwen3's eight expository failures are more distributed (4~early, 1~mid, 3~late), suggesting that the relationship between task type and divergence timing is itself architecture-dependent.

\section{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Architecture sensitivity.} The strongest practical result appears on the tested MoE models. The magnitude of adaptive-depth gains is architecture-dependent, as shown by the quality difference between GPT-OSS 20B (0.870) and Qwen3-30B-A3B (0.837).

\item \textbf{MoE coverage.} The cross-model sparse-MoE validation covers two families. Broader MoE generalization remains untested.

\item \textbf{Track B scope.} The cascade result is specific to a naive implementation, one model pair, and one local runtime. It should not be generalized to speculative decoding as a class of methods.

\item \textbf{Runtime dependence.} All throughput, latency, and memory measurements are specific to a single Apple M1 Pro system using MLX. Direct comparison to GPU-based inference is not appropriate.

\item \textbf{Evaluation scale.} Track~A uses 63 scored prompts across six categories; Tracks~B and~C use five. This is sufficient for directional findings but insufficient for statistical confidence intervals.

\item \textbf{Dense selective-depth.} Real selective-depth runtimes on all three tested dense families were operationally correct but did not recover a practical speed-quality frontier in this configuration.

\item \textbf{Continuation mechanisms.} The current selective-depth results demonstrate that checkpoint placement and late-checkpoint confidence alone are insufficient. Better continuation criteria, token-difficulty-aware routing, and lower-overhead cache repair remain open problems.

\item \textbf{Family-sensitive structure.} The benchmark-side dense limitation generalizes across tested families, but the internal KL-profile shape does not. We do not claim a single universal dense depth profile.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

The evidence presented supports a narrow but consistent conclusion: adaptive-depth inference for transformers is constrained less by exit-point selection than by cross-layer composition control---how intermediate and full-depth signals are combined without corrupting the output distribution.

Track~A remains the primary empirical result. On GPT-OSS 20B, \texttt{top1\_agree} at the penultimate layer achieves 0.870 exact match (47 of 63 perfect), with approximately 49\% of decode tokens physically skipping the final MoE-MLP block. On Qwen3-30B-A3B, the same mechanism at the penultimate layer achieves 0.837 exact match (36 of 63 perfect) with a sharp quality cliff one layer earlier. The frontier structure reproduces across both tested sparse MoE families; the operating-point quality is model-dependent. Track~B provides a scoped negative baseline for this naive cascade configuration. Track~C contributes three findings: fixed-exit failure on dense models, strong compute-both composition recovery, and weak real selective-depth across three tested families.

The dense-model conclusion is not that adaptive depth is impossible, but that neither fixed exit nor current selective-depth mechanisms establish a practical frontier on the tested dense architectures. The next research direction is correspondingly narrower: whether family-sensitive continuation criteria, token-difficulty-aware routing, and cache-aware continuation can close the gap. That remains to be validated.
